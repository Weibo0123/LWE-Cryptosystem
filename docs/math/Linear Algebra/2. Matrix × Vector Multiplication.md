## **Matrix × Vector Multiplication**

### **Algebraic Formula**

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix and a vector:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
A & B \\
C & D
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
Ax + By \\
Cx + Dy
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This expression gives the standard computational rule.

However, this rule follows naturally from geometry rather than memorization.

---

## **2. Conceptual**

### **Matrix as Columns**

Write the matrix as columns:

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
A & B \\
C & D
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
c_1 \; c_2
\end{bmatrix}
$$

where

$$
c_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
A \\
C
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
c_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
B \\
D
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### **Interpretation**

- first column → image of basis vector $e_1$
- second column → image of basis vector $e_2$

Thus:

- columns represent the **transformed basis vectors**
- the matrix defines a **linear transformation**

---

### **Transforming a Vector**

Let:

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Geometric meaning:

- move $x$ units in direction $c_1$
- move $y$ units in direction $c_2$

Therefore:

$$
Ax = x c_1 + y c_2
$$

---

### **Expanding the Linear Combination**

$$
x
\begin{bmatrix}
A \\
C
\end{bmatrix}+
y
\begin{bmatrix}
B \\
D
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
Ax + By \\
Cx + Dy
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Therefore:

$$
Ax =
\begin{bmatrix}
Ax + By \\
Cx + Dy
\end{bmatrix}
$$

---

## **3. Computational**

An equivalent formula:

$$
(Ax)_i = \sum_j a_{ij} x_j
$$


Each row computes a **dot product** with the vector.

---

### **Row-Based Example**

First row:

$$
[A \; B]
\cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
=
Ax + By
$$

Second row:

$$
[C \; D]
\cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
=
Cx + Dy
$$

Thus, each output entry equals one row–vector dot product.
