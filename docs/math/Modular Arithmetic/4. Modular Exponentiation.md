## **1. Modular Exponentiation**

Modular exponentiation is based on the multiplication property of modular arithmetic:

$$
(A \cdot B) \bmod C = (A \bmod C \cdot B \bmod C) \bmod C
$$

This property allows exponentiation to be broken into smaller, manageable steps.

---

## **2. Divide-and-Conquer for Exponents**

Using exponent rules:

$$
A^{m+n} = A^m \cdot A^n
$$

Applying modulo $C$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
A^B \bmod C
&= (A^m \cdot A^n) \bmod C \\
&= (A^m \bmod C \cdot A^n \bmod C) \bmod C
\end{aligned}
$$

This allows large exponents to be decomposed into smaller parts.

---

## **3. Example: Decomposing an Exponent**

$$
2^{90} = 2^{50} \cdot 2^{40}
$$

Compute:

$$
2^{50} \bmod 13, \quad 2^{40} \bmod 13
$$

Then multiply the results and apply modulo $13$ again.

This avoids computing $2^{90}$ directly.

---

## **4. Fast Modular Exponentiation**

Repeated squaring is used to efficiently compute powers.

$$
\begin{aligned}
A^2 \bmod C &= (A \cdot A) \bmod C \\
A^4 \bmod C &= (A^2)^2 \bmod C \\
A^8 \bmod C &= (A^4)^2 \bmod C \\
A^{16} \bmod C &= (A^8)^2 \bmod C
\end{aligned}
$$

In general:

$$
A^{2^k} \bmod C = (A^{2^{k-1}} \bmod C)^2 \bmod C
$$

Each step consists only of:

- Squaring
- Taking modulo $C$

Intermediate values remain small.

---

## **5. Example: Compute $7^{256} \bmod 13$**

Since:

$$
256 = 2^8
$$

Apply repeated squaring:

$$
\begin{aligned}
7^1 \bmod 13 &= 7 \\
7^2 \bmod 13 &= 49 \bmod 13 = 10 \\
7^4 \bmod 13 &= 10^2 \bmod 13 = 100 \bmod 13 = 9 \\
7^8 \bmod 13 &= 9^2 \bmod 13 = 81 \bmod 13 = 3 \\
7^{16} \bmod 13 &= 3^2 \bmod 13 = 9 \\
7^{32} \bmod 13 &= 9^2 \bmod 13 = 3 \\
7^{64} \bmod 13 &= 3^2 \bmod 13 = 9 \\
7^{128} \bmod 13 &= 9^2 \bmod 13 = 3 \\
7^{256} \bmod 13 &= 3^2 \bmod 13 = \mathbf{9}
\end{aligned}
$$
