## **1. Modular Inverse**

### **Definition**

In modular arithmetic, division is not defined, but **modular inverses** are.

The modular inverse of $A$ modulo $C$ is a number $A^{-1}$ such that:

$$
A \cdot A^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{C}
$$

Equivalently:

$$
(A \cdot A^{-1}) \bmod C = 1
$$

Only integers **coprime** to $C$ have a modular inverse modulo $C$.

---

## **2. Existence Condition**

An integer $A$ has a modular inverse modulo $C$ **if and only if**:

$$
\gcd(A, C) = 1
$$

That is:

$$
A^{-1} \text{ exists mod } C \iff \gcd(A, C) = 1
$$

---

## **3. Naive Method for Finding a Modular Inverse**

To find the modular inverse of $A \pmod{C}$:

### **Step 1**

Compute:

$$
A \cdot B \bmod C
$$

for:

$$
B = 0, 1, 2, \dots, C - 1
$$

### **Step 2**

The value of $B$ that satisfies:

$$
A \cdot B \equiv 1 \pmod{C}
$$

is the modular inverse of $A$ modulo $C$.

Testing values beyond $C-1$ is unnecessary, since all residues modulo $C$ lie in $\{0,1,\dots,C-1\}$.

---

## **4. Why a Modular Inverse Requires $\gcd(A, C) = 1$**

### **Intuitive Argument**

Assume $A$ and $C$ share a common divisor $d > 1$:

$$
A = da, \quad C = dc
$$

For any integer $B$:

$$
A \cdot B = d(aB)
$$

so $A \cdot B$ is always divisible by $d$.

If a modular inverse existed, then for some $B$:

$$
A \cdot B \equiv 1 \pmod{C}
$$

which implies:

$$
A \cdot B = 1 + kC = 1 + k(dc)
$$

The right-hand side is **not divisible by $d$**, while the left-hand side **is divisible by $d$**.

This is a contradiction.

Therefore, if:

$$
\gcd(A, C) \ne 1
$$

no modular inverse exists.

---

## **5. Bézout’s Identity Interpretation**

Bézout’s identity states:

$$
\exists x, y \in \mathbb{Z} \text{ such that } Ax + Cy = 1
\iff \gcd(A, C) = 1
$$

Taking both sides modulo $C$:

$$
Ax + Cy \equiv Ax \pmod{C}
$$

since:

$$
Cy \equiv 0 \pmod{C}
$$

Thus:

$$
Ax \equiv 1 \pmod{C}
$$

Hence, $x$ is the modular inverse of $A$ modulo $C$.

Therefore:

$$
A^{-1} \text{ exists mod } C \iff \gcd(A, C) = 1
$$
