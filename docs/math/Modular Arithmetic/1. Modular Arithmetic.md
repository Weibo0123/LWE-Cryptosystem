## **Modular Arithmetic**

When dividing two integers, the division algorithm gives:

$$
\frac{A}{B} = Q \text{ remainder } R
$$

Where:

- $A$ is the **dividend**
- $B$ is the **divisor**
- $Q$ is the **quotient**
- $R$ is the **remainder**

In many contexts, only the **remainder** is relevant.

---

## **2. Modulo Operation**

The **modulo operator** (mod) returns the remainder of a division.

Using the same notation as above:

$$
A \bmod B = R
$$

Terminology:

- $A \bmod B$ is read as “$A$ modulo $B$”
- $B$ is called the **modulus**

### **Example**

$$
\frac{13}{5} = 2 \text{ remainder } 3
$$

$$
13 \bmod 5 = 3
$$

---

## **3. Congruence Modulo**

An expression of the form:

$$
A \equiv B \; (\text{mod } C)
$$

means that $A$ is **congruent** to $B$ modulo $C$.

Interpretation:

1. $\equiv$ denotes **congruence**, meaning $A$ and $B$ belong to the same **equivalence class**.
2. $(\text{mod } C)$ specifies the modulus used.
3. Together, $A \equiv B \pmod{C}$ is called **congruence modulo $C$**.

---

## **4. Equivalent Characterizations**

The following statements are **equivalent**:

- $A \equiv B \pmod{C}$
- $A \bmod C = B \bmod C$
- $C \mid (A - B)$
- $A = B + K \cdot C$, where $K \in \mathbb{Z}$

---

## **5. Congruence as an Equivalence Relation**

Congruence modulo $C$ is an **equivalence relation**.

### **Properties of Equivalence Relations**

An equivalence relation satisfies:

- **Reflexive**: $A$ is related to $A$
- **Symmetric**: if $A$ is related to $B$, then $B$ is related to $A$
- **Transitive**: if $A$ is related to $B$ and $B$ is related to $D$, then $A$ is related to $D$

---

## **6. Consequences for Congruence Modulo $C$**

Since congruence modulo $C$ is an equivalence relation:

- $A \equiv A \pmod{C}$
- If $A \equiv B \pmod{C}$, then $B \equiv A \pmod{C}$
- If $A \equiv B \pmod{C}$ and $B \equiv D \pmod{C}$, then $A \equiv D \pmod{C}$
